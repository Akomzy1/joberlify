An "ATS-optimised CV" has become one of the most searched phrases in the job search world — and one of the most misunderstood. There's an entire industry of tools and advice built around gaming these systems, most of which is either wrong or counterproductive.

This guide explains what ATS systems actually do, what genuinely improves your chances of passing them, and what advice you should ignore.

## What ATS Actually Does (and What It Doesn't)

**Applicant Tracking System (ATS)** is a broad term for software used by employers to receive, sort, and manage job applications. The term covers hundreds of different products — Greenhouse, Workday, Taleo, Lever, SmartRecruiters, iCIMS — that work very differently from each other.

What most ATS systems do:
- **Store** your application data in a searchable database
- **Parse** your CV into structured fields (name, contact, employment history, education, skills)
- **Allow recruiters to filter** applications by keyword, location, qualification, or other criteria

What most ATS systems do not do:
- **Automatically score** your CV and reject you without human review (this is rare outside very high-volume roles)
- **Read complex formatting** — tables, text boxes, headers/footers, and columns often parse incorrectly
- **Understand synonyms** unless specifically configured (some do, most don't)

The practical implication: the biggest risk is not that an algorithm rejects you — it's that your CV **fails to parse correctly** and a recruiter searching for "Python" or "project management" never sees your application because those words were trapped in a table that the system couldn't read.

## The Formatting Rules That Actually Matter

### Use a single-column layout

Two-column CVs are popular with graphic designers and template marketers. They look professional when viewed in PDF. They are frequently mangled by ATS parsing — skills listed in a sidebar may be read as part of a random paragraph elsewhere.

Single-column layouts parse reliably across every ATS in use. This is the single most impactful formatting decision you can make.

### Avoid tables for critical content

A table summarising your skills sounds efficient. In practice, many ATS parsers extract table contents in unpredictable ways — row by row, column by column, or not at all. If your skills are in a table and the parser skips it, a recruiter filtering for "Java" will never find your application.

Use bullet points for skills lists. They parse universally.

### Use standard section headings

ATS parsers look for specific headings to categorise your content. "Work History" may not be recognised; "Professional Experience" or "Work Experience" almost certainly will be. The same applies to "Education" (always works) vs. "Academic Background" (sometimes doesn't).

Safe headings: Work Experience, Professional Experience, Education, Skills, Certifications, Awards, Summary, Objective.

### Avoid headers and footers for contact information

Contact details placed in a Word or PDF header/footer are invisible to many parsers. Your email address and phone number should be in the body of the document, not the document header.

### File format: PDF is usually correct, but check

Most modern ATS systems handle PDF well. Some older enterprise systems (particularly Taleo) still struggle with PDFs and prefer .docx. When in doubt, and when the application form doesn't specify, PDF is the safer choice for formatting consistency.

## Keyword Strategy: What Works and What Doesn't

### Use the exact language from the job description

Every job description uses specific vocabulary. A role that says "stakeholder management" wants to see "stakeholder management" in your CV — not "working with internal clients" or "relationship building," even if those phrases mean the same thing to a human reader.

The practical approach: after reading a job description, identify the five to ten most specific phrases used to describe required skills, and ensure your CV uses those exact phrases (where they honestly describe your experience).

### Target skills density, not keyword stuffing

Keyword stuffing — listing 50 skills in a single paragraph to maximise matches — is detectable and off-putting to human reviewers. More importantly, most ATS systems weight *specificity* not frequency. Mentioning "machine learning" twice in context is more credible than mentioning it eight times in a skills dump.

Focus on using key phrases naturally in descriptions of actual work you have done.

### Don't add skills you don't have

ATS optimisation services sometimes suggest adding keywords for skills you haven't used. This passes the filter — and fails the interview. Hiring managers recognise when a candidate's interview answers don't match CV claims.

## The Content Rules That Matter

### Lead with impact, not responsibilities

Most CVs describe what the candidate was supposed to do. Strong CVs describe what actually happened as a result of their work.

Weak: "Responsible for managing social media accounts for the brand."
Strong: "Grew Instagram following from 12,000 to 47,000 in 14 months through a weekly content series; engagement rate 3.2× industry benchmark."

ATS systems don't evaluate impact — but hiring managers do, immediately after the CV passes the filter.

### Quantify wherever possible

Numbers outperform adjectives. "Significantly improved" is ignored; "reduced processing time by 34%" is specific and credible. Even approximate numbers are better than none — "managed a team of around 8 engineers" is stronger than "managed a small engineering team."

### Keep it to two pages (most roles)

For roles below senior director / partner level, a two-page CV is the standard. One page for graduates and early-career candidates. Three or more pages are appropriate only for academic CVs (CVs for research, teaching, and professorial roles, which have different conventions).

## Common Mistakes That ATS Optimisation Cannot Fix

The following mistakes will hurt your applications regardless of how well-optimised your CV is:

**Unexplained employment gaps** — ATS passes you; the recruiter then asks about the gap. Have a clear, honest answer.

**No evidence of results** — Keyword-matching a job description doesn't compensate for a CV that lists responsibilities without outcomes.

**Generic profile summary** — "A highly motivated professional with excellent communication skills" is present on enough CVs that it has become meaningless. Replace it with a specific statement about your expertise and what you're looking for.

**Inconsistent dates** — ATS parsers flag date inconsistencies as potential red flags. Ensure month and year formatting is consistent throughout.

**Applying to roles you're genuinely unqualified for** — No amount of keyword optimisation will help a junior candidate pass a hiring manager's review for a role requiring ten years of specific experience.

## A Note on AI CV Generators

Generative AI tools can now produce a tailored CV draft based on a job description and a prompt. The output quality varies significantly:

- Good AI CV tools: produce a realistic, coherent narrative based on actual experience you describe; suggest specific phrasings from the job description; flag gaps between your experience and the requirements
- Poor AI CV tools: hallucinate achievements you didn't claim; produce generic, inflated language that sounds nothing like your actual voice; ignore your actual experience in favour of keyword density

## Summary

- ATS parsing errors (not algorithmic rejection) are the main formatting risk; use single-column layouts and avoid tables
- Use exact language from the job description for skills and requirements
- Lead with results and numbers, not responsibilities and duties
- Two pages is standard for most roles; one page for early-career candidates
- No ATS optimisation compensates for being genuinely unqualified or for hollow application narratives

The goal isn't to pass a computer — it's to get in front of a hiring manager who is interested in what you've actually done.
