If you need a UK work visa to take a job, you need an employer who is willing and legally authorised to sponsor you. This guide explains exactly how the system works, what you need to check before applying, and how to find the right opportunities without wasting months on dead-end applications.

## How UK Visa Sponsorship Actually Works

The UK Skilled Worker visa replaced the Tier 2 (General) visa in December 2020. Under this system, an employer cannot simply decide to hire an overseas worker — they must first hold a **sponsor licence** granted by the Home Office.

To sponsor a worker, the employer must:

1. Hold a valid A-rated sponsor licence
2. Assign the worker a **Certificate of Sponsorship (CoS)** — a unique reference number tied to the specific role
3. Pay the **Immigration Skills Charge** (£1,000 per year for large employers, £364 per year for small employers and charities)
4. Ensure the role meets both the **skill level** (RQF Level 3+) and **salary threshold** requirements

The employee then applies for the visa using the CoS reference number. The process takes a minimum of three weeks from application submission, though most are decided within eight weeks.

## The Three-Layer Eligibility Check

When evaluating whether a job will support a Skilled Worker visa, there are three separate questions you must answer. Many job seekers only check the first one.

### Layer 1: Is the employer on the sponsor register?

The Home Office publishes the **Register of Licensed Sponsors** — a list of every UK employer authorised to issue Certificates of Sponsorship. This register currently contains over 120,000 organisations.

You can search this register at GOV.UK, or use Joberlify's sponsor directory which syncs the register weekly. A word of warning: presence on the register is necessary but not sufficient. You also need to check the employer's rating.

### Layer 2: Is the employer A-rated?

Every sponsor on the register carries one of two ratings:

- **A-rated**: The employer is in good standing and can issue new Certificates of Sponsorship immediately. This is what you want.
- **B-rated**: The employer has been placed on a formal action plan and **cannot issue new Certificates of Sponsorship** until it regains A status. A job offer from a B-rated employer will not currently lead to a successful visa application.

B-rating typically occurs after a Home Office compliance audit reveals issues with record-keeping, right-to-work checks, or sponsorship duties. Many B-rated employers are in the process of rectifying these issues, but the timeline is uncertain.

Always verify the rating before investing time in an application.

### Layer 3: Does the role meet the skill and salary thresholds?

Even if the employer is A-rated, the specific **role** must qualify under the immigration rules. This requires two checks:

**Skill level**: The role must be classified under a SOC 2020 occupation code that appears on the eligible occupations list. Most roles at RQF Level 3 (A-level equivalent) or above qualify.

**Salary threshold**: The advertised salary must meet both:
- The **general threshold**: currently £38,700 per year (as of April 2024, subject to annual review)
- The **going rate** for the specific SOC code — which in many technical and medical occupations is higher than the general threshold

For new entrants (those in their first five years post-graduation or similar), a lower rate applies (currently 70% of the going rate), but the general threshold floor still applies.

## Step-by-Step: How to Find Sponsorship-Eligible Jobs

### Step 1: Search the sponsor register first

Before spending time on any job application, verify the employer is on the register and is A-rated. This is a five-second check that eliminates a significant percentage of wasted applications.

### Step 2: Identify the SOC code for the role

Every job title maps to a SOC 2020 occupation code. Some titles map ambiguously — "Software Engineer" might map to 2136 (Software Developer) or 2133 (IT specialist) depending on responsibilities. The salary threshold may differ between codes.

Joberlify automatically classifies job descriptions to the correct SOC code as part of its full visa eligibility check.

### Step 3: Verify the salary

Check whether the advertised salary (or the typical salary for the role if not advertised) meets both the general threshold and the going rate for the SOC code. If the advert says "competitive" without a figure, you will need to research typical salaries or ask at the interview stage.

### Step 4: Check recent hire patterns

Some employers on the sponsor register sponsor frequently; others have a licence but rarely use it. If a company's job postings consistently don't mention visa sponsorship, they may be technically licensed but unwilling in practice. LinkedIn and Glassdoor reviews sometimes indicate whether a company actively sponsors.

### Step 5: Surface the topic appropriately

During the application process, if a role doesn't mention visa sponsorship, it is better to raise the topic after receiving a positive response (first-stage interview, recruiter call) rather than in your cover letter. Mentioning it too early can filter you out before the hiring manager has seen your application.

## What the Home Office Considers When Assessing Applications

Understanding the Home Office's perspective helps you frame your application correctly:

- **Genuine vacancy**: The role must be a real, ongoing vacancy (not created solely to sponsor a worker)
- **Salary compliance**: The actual offered salary must meet the threshold — not just the advertised salary
- **English language**: You must demonstrate English language proficiency at B1 level or above
- **Maintenance funds**: You must show you can support yourself during the initial period (usually £1,270 held for 28 consecutive days)
- **Criminal record**: Serious criminal convictions can disqualify an application

## Frequently Asked Questions About UK Visa Sponsorship

**Can a company sponsor me if they're not on the register?** No. To sponsor a Skilled Worker visa, an employer must hold a valid sponsor licence. A company can apply for a licence (typically taking 8 weeks to process), but they cannot sponsor you during the application period.

**How long does a Skilled Worker visa last?** Up to five years. After five years of continuous residence, you can apply for Indefinite Leave to Remain (ILR), which is the first step towards permanent settlement.

**Can I change employers on a Skilled Worker visa?** Yes, but your new employer must also be a licensed sponsor. You will need a new Certificate of Sponsorship before you can switch roles. There is no cooling-off period, but you should not start the new role until you have a decision on your new visa application (unless your existing visa has more than three months remaining).

**Does my employer pay for the visa?** The Certificate of Sponsorship fee is paid by the employer. The visa application fee is typically paid by the applicant (though some employers reimburse this). The Immigration Health Surcharge (currently £1,035 per year) is paid by the applicant.

**What if I'm offered less than the threshold?** The role does not qualify for a Skilled Worker visa at that salary, regardless of your qualifications. You would need to negotiate a higher salary, find a role that maps to a lower-threshold SOC code, or find an employer willing to pay the required amount.
