If you're applying for a UK Skilled Worker visa — or trying to understand whether a job offer will support one — you've likely encountered the term "SOC code." It's one of the most important and least-explained concepts in the UK immigration system.

This guide explains what SOC codes are, why they matter for visa eligibility, how to find the right code for your role, and how to handle the most common edge cases.

## What Is a SOC Code?

SOC stands for **Standard Occupational Classification**. It is a system developed by the UK Office for National Statistics (ONS) to categorise every type of paid work into a structured hierarchy.

The current version is **SOC 2020**, which replaced SOC 2010 for immigration purposes on 1 January 2024. If you've seen references to the older four-digit codes from SOC 2010, note that many of these have changed — some significantly — in the 2020 version.

The SOC 2020 hierarchy has four levels:

- **Major group** (1 digit): e.g., 2 — Professional occupations
- **Sub-major group** (2 digits): e.g., 21 — Science, research, engineering and technology professionals
- **Minor group** (3 digits): e.g., 213 — IT professionals
- **Unit group** (4 digits): e.g., 2136 — Programmers and software development professionals

For visa purposes, the relevant level is always the **4-digit unit group**.

## Why SOC Codes Matter for Visa Eligibility

The UK Home Office uses SOC codes to determine:

1. **Whether a role qualifies** at all for a Skilled Worker visa (the role must fall under an eligible occupation code)
2. **What salary threshold applies** — specifically, the "going rate" for that occupation code
3. **Whether new entrant rates apply** (70% of the going rate for recent graduates and early-career workers)

Two people with similar job titles may be classified under different SOC codes depending on their actual responsibilities, which means they may face different salary thresholds and different eligibility criteria. Getting the SOC code wrong can lead to visa refusal even when the employer is a licensed sponsor and the salary appears adequate.

## How SOC Codes Are Assigned

SOC codes are not self-selected by employers or applicants. They are determined by the actual content of the work — specifically:

- The **tasks** performed day-to-day
- The **qualifications** typically required
- The **level of responsibility** involved

The Home Office guidance provides occupation descriptions for each unit group. When an employer assigns a Certificate of Sponsorship, they must select the SOC code that best matches the actual role — not the job title. A company can call a role "Growth Manager," but if it involves primarily digital marketing activities, it should map to the marketing manager codes rather than a general management code.

Employers are responsible for correct classification. However, errors are common, and the Home Office can question a CoS if the assigned code doesn't match the salary or role description.

## Roles That Do NOT Qualify

Not all roles qualify for a Skilled Worker visa. The Home Office publishes a list of eligible occupation codes — if a role's SOC code is not on the list, no sponsorship is possible regardless of salary.

Common roles that are **not** eligible include:

- Most retail sales and customer service roles (SOC 7xxx groups)
- Most routine manual occupations
- Basic administration roles below RQF Level 3
- Lorry and van drivers (though this has been subject to policy changes)

Additionally, even if a SOC code is on the eligible list, if the advertised salary falls below the threshold for that specific code, the visa application will be refused.

## How to Find the Right SOC Code for Your Role

### Method 1: ONS SOC 2020 lookup tool

The Office for National Statistics provides a search tool at ons.gov.uk where you can search by job title or task description. This is the authoritative reference but can be slow to navigate.

### Method 2: Home Office Appendix Skilled Occupations

The immigration rules contain a full list of eligible SOC codes with their going rates. This is the definitive source for visa purposes. It is updated when the rules change (usually in April each year).

## New Entrant Rates

For some applicants, the going rate can be reduced to **70% of the standard figure**. You qualify as a "new entrant" if:

- You are switching from a Student visa (and it has been less than 3 years since you completed your studies)
- You are under 26 at the time of application
- You are in the first year of a recognised PhD programme
- You are in the first year of a UK Postgraduate Certificate of Education (PGCE)

The new entrant rate cannot fall below the general threshold floor (currently £38,700 per year). So if 70% of the going rate for a code is less than £38,700, the floor applies.

## Summary

- SOC codes classify occupations for immigration purposes; the correct 4-digit code determines visa eligibility and salary threshold
- The current system is SOC 2020, which replaced SOC 2010 in January 2024
- Not all occupations are eligible — the role must appear on the Home Office's eligible occupations list
- Salary must meet both the general threshold (£38,700 for 2024 onwards) and the code-specific going rate
- Correct SOC classification is the employer's responsibility but errors are common; Joberlify automates this check

Understanding your SOC code before applying for roles requiring sponsorship will save you significant time and frustration. Knowing the going rate for your classification also gives you useful leverage in salary negotiations.
